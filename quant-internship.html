Summer in Review

Another sun-soaked season comes to a close. Previous summers revolved around research, so I was happy to “break” into finance this year. It’s been a tremendously interesting internship, owing to some unusual circumstances and a fantastic team. I worked in a large commercial bank at the top floor (well, the C-Suite occupies the floor above us) and it was a thrill walking through downtown to get to work every morning. I’ll be honest - pressing my floor in the elevator still hasn’t gotten old after 10 weeks.

A full-time hire started the same day as me and we were both assigned to the same portfolio. Working with him was a learning opportunity in and of itself; he was incredibly proactive with great interpersonal skills, and served as a good example for how to act as a new hire. We both hit the ground running from the first week.

Every intern also got a mentor. Mine was a former math professor and one of my former interviewers. Out of three people, he had asked the toughest technical questions.

More Technical…

Our work revolves around implementing Basel III, which is an international regulatory framework for banks that seeks to establish good banking practices for maintaining adequate capital, stress testing our models, and managing market liquidity risk.

My group focused on capital adequacy. For each of the bank’s portfolios, we would model the probability of default (PD) and the loss given default (LGD). Those two models along with the exposure at default (EAD) would give us the expected loss for a given portfolio. Subprime Mortgages

The asset class that my internship focused on was mortgages and in particular, I spent a lot of time on the subprime mortgage portfolio. Older people will be familiar with this; the subprime mortgage crisis is what caused the 2007-2009 economic recession. I was still a kid at the time and before this internship, I barely understood what a mortgage was, let alone the factors that went into causing the financial crisis. A friend recently recommended The Big Short to me, offers an educational and funny look into how subprime mortgages (and mortgage-backed securities) did the damage it did.
