Operation HEAR

Last updated: 8/25/2016

Since I was a kid, I’ve wished for a way to hear while in the water. As of 2016, there is only one waterproof hearing aid on the market and even then, it is only for people with up to 65dB hearing loss. That counts me out.

This is my journal of trying to figure something out anyway :)

12/29/2015

Got fitted with Resound Enzo2. This hearing aid has actually been life-changing. It is Made for iPhone® and routes sound from my phone directly into my hearing aid’s internal microphone via Bluetooth. For the first time, I could Skype and not have to ask my friends to constantly repeat themselves because the max volume wasn’t loud enough.

8/05/2016

Went tubing with my friends! I had taken out my hearing aids for tubing in a lake before, but this trip was a 3-hour trip down a lazy river. We would essentially be chilling and talking the entire time. I didn’t want to be left out so I wore my old pair from high school. I figured that if something happened to them, I’d still have my pair from undergrad and the ReSounds. The trip turned out to be a blast, my friends tied up our tubes together and blasted music while talking.

Image from Trinity River Vision Authority.

8/23/2016

Hatched crazy idea of using iPhone as a microphone and pairing it to waterproof bluetooth headphones. Enclose iPhone in a waterproof case and swim in it. I had already gotten the case during tubing and discovered that I could still talk on the phone. My phone’s internal microphone could pick up sound even in a watertight case. Of course, this depends on the case actually working and me not destroying my phone in the process.

Preliminary Research

Bluetooth is a wireless technology standard that operates on ultra high frequency radio waves in the ISM band from 2.4 to 2.485GHz. In essence, it transmits signals through electromagnetic radiation but things get tricky when moving from air to water. Signal loss can occur throughs:

  • transmission loss from reflection and refraction at the air/water boundary
  • propagation loss due to water’s high permittivity and electrical conductivity

I think the first case shouldn’t be an issue since my headphones would already be in the water. That leaves the second case. Water’s high permittivity reduces any electric field around it, which is problematic since electromagnetic waves propagate via disturbances in the electric (and magnetic) fields.

-> Image from Physics Classroom <-

Radio frequencies like Bluetooth suffer from high attenuation - its intensity decreases sharply upon contact with water molecules. Rhodes explains this process,

Loss is largely due to the effect of conduction on the electric field component. Propagating waves continually cycle energy between the electric and magnetic fields; hence conduction leads to strong attenuation of electromagnetic propagating waves.

Saltwater has a high conductivity while freshwater has a relatively low conductivity. This means that trying to engineer a hearing solution for an activity like snorkeling will be more challenging than swimming in a lake.

It turns out that Bluetooth has virtually nil connectivity. Jiang and Georgakopoulos (2011) show that the optimum frequency for air-to-water communication is short-wave radiowaves in the 3 to 100 MHz range. In contrast, Bluetooth is almost two orders of magnitude bigger, falling under the spectrum of microwaves. Anyone who’s had a good middle school science teacher will remember that kitchen microwaves heats food by heating the water molecules, which absorbs the microwaves.

Wireless frequencies that do work underwater include sonar and NFC. Apple implemented NFC chips in iPhone 6 for Apple Pay, but have banned access by third party apps. Regardless, it’s a moot point since NFC headphones are so few and not at all waterproof. If I hope to use my phone, bluetooth it’ll have to be.

For my purposes, I don’t really need a big range. If I am swimming with waterproof earbuds, then that’s just a depth of one feet. I spent a lot of time looking at forums for R/C submarines and… I am actually kinda appalled by how much misinformation there was. Some posters would attribute the ineffectiveness of Bluetooth to the resonant frequency of water, which is incorrect. I didn’t know anything just a few days ago, but it is problematic when veteran posters propagate incorrect information to newbies. Luckily, this is the Internet, and there is always somebody rushing to prove people wrong.

In the meantime, it is REALLY hard to find published results about bluetooth range in water. You would think this would be a common topic, but there’s nada. I guess that it’s just so ineffective that there was no point in truly measuring it. I have about two weeks left of summer vacation and I PROMISED myself I wouldn’t do any math, but here we go…

Just how far can bluetooth travel underwater?
